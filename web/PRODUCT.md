# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

Greensboro Fire Department training officers and evaluators are the primary users. They operate the app hands-on while proctoring a recruit through a practical skills test — often outdoors, in daylight, sometimes with gloves on or wet hands, and under a running clock. Their job on any given screen is to grade the step in front of them correctly and move on without fumbling. A secondary set of users are administrators who build tests, manage recruits and users, and read reporting after the fact, working in calmer conditions on a desktop or iPad. Recruits themselves are the subjects of the testing, not operators of the app.

## Product Purpose

The app runs recruit practical skills tests for the fire department: an admin builds tests (graded steps, timers, obstacle courses, sequential test groups), an evaluator takes a recruit through a test live and records pass/fail per step against a percentage-based passing score, and the results roll up into per-recruit history and cohort readiness reporting. Success is an evaluator getting through a timed test without a single mis-tap or moment of hesitation, and a training officer being able to see at a glance who is ready and who is flagged.

## Positioning

The purpose-built grading tool for live fire-department practical tests — fast and unambiguous in the field, with reporting that turns a day of testing into a readable picture of cohort readiness.

## Brand Personality

Sharp and operational. The app should read like dependable fire-service equipment: decisive, high-legibility, unhesitating. Confidence comes from clarity and speed, not decoration. Language is direct and instrument-like — an action says exactly what it does and keeps the same name through the flow. It carries the department's institutional navy/gold/red identity, but the personality is in how quickly a decision can be read and made, not in styling flourish.

## Anti-references

Not a consumer or gamified app: no mascots, badges, streaks, confetti, or candy colors — a recruit passing or failing a fire test is serious, and the interface treats it that way. Equally, not dense enterprise or government software: no cramped gray tables, no tiny text, no 1990s admin-panel density. The failure mode on one side is "toy," on the other side "unusable form"; the app lives between them — spare and instrument-like, never either.

## Design Principles

Legible at a glance, under pressure. Every state an evaluator needs mid-test — whose turn, which step, pass or fail, time remaining — must be readable in one look, in sunlight, without leaning in. Contrast and size win over elegance whenever they conflict.

Every control is glove-ready. Tap targets stay large and forgiving; nothing requires precision or a steady hand. A misread or a mis-tap during a timed test is a real cost, so hit areas are generous and destructive actions are guarded.

Signal redundantly, never by color alone. Pass and fail — and any other status that carries weight — always pair color with text or an icon, so a color-blind evaluator or a washed-out screen never changes the reading.

Neither toy nor terminal. Hold the line between gamified-consumer polish and cramped-gov-software density. When a screen feels like it's drifting toward either pole, pull it back to spare and operational.

Speed over spectacle. Nothing decorative may slow a timed test or a quick grading decision. Motion and visual richness earn their place only when they make a state easier to read; otherwise they're cut.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Designed for outdoor use in bright daylight: high contrast and large text are treated as functional requirements, not stylistic preferences. Interactive controls are sized and spaced for gloved or wet hands (generous targets, forgiving spacing). Status that matters — especially pass/fail — is never conveyed by green/red alone; it is always reinforced with text or an icon so it survives color blindness and glare.
